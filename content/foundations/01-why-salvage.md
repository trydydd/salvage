This page covers why component salvage is worth the time, what a realistic haul actually looks like, and how the rest of the guide fits together so you can find what you need.

## Why reuse at all

Salvaged parts are free, already in your hands, and often better made than the cheap equivalents you'd order online. A drawer of pulled electrolytics, connectors, and switches means you can start a repair or a build tonight instead of waiting a week for a parts order with a minimum charge attached.

Cost is the obvious draw, but it isn't the main one for most people who keep doing this. The real return is what you learn taking things apart. After your tenth power supply you can read the layout at a glance: here's the mains side, here's the switcher, here's where the cheap caps always fail. That feel for how things are built makes you faster at repair, better at design, and harder to fool when something's sold as broken.

Availability matters too. Old or odd parts, a particular connector, a mains transformer, a stepper motor, often aren't worth a supplier's time to stock, but they fall out of a dead printer or a broken appliance for nothing. Salvage is sometimes the only practical source for the exact thing you need.

And some parts are simply good. The Japanese electrolytics in a name-brand PSU, the JST connectors in a laptop, the gear trains in a printer, are a step up from the bargain versions you'd buy. Pulling them is an upgrade, not a compromise.

## Realistic expectations

Most donor devices give up a handful of genuinely useful parts, not a full parts store. A dead inkjet might be worth two stepper motors, a few rollers, and a sheet-metal shaft, and the rest is plastic and proprietary boards you'll never reuse. That's a good haul. Counting parts is the wrong measure.

Judge a teardown by function, not volume. One good connector you actually needed beats a tray of resistors you'll never sort through. A 12 V brushless fan that still spins true is worth more than a board crowded with surface-mount parts you have no way to test. Ask what you can identify, test, and find again later, and keep those.

A lot of what you pull, you'll throw away again eventually. Ceramic caps and jellybean resistors are so cheap new that recovering them rarely pays for the desoldering time, unless you're practicing. Knowing what to leave on the board is as useful as knowing what to take.

Expect the occasional empty teardown too. Some devices are all custom silicon and glued-down assemblies with nothing worth the effort. Opening one and deciding there's nothing here is a valid result, not a failure.

## The salvage mindset

Open things to see how they work, not just to strip them. The habit that separates people who get good at this from people who end up with a box of mystery parts is curiosity about the device in front of them. Notice why the heatsink is oversized, or what killed this particular unit. The answers make you better at the next one.

Write down what you find while the board is still in front of you. A part number photographed in place, a connector traced to where it went, a cap's voltage rating noted before it joins fifty others in a bag, all of that is easy now and impossible later. The markings are the part's whole identity, and they're often unreadable once it's loose.

Sort as you go, by what you can actually verify. A part you've tested and labeled is inventory. A part in an unlabeled box is landfill you're storing. The components pages cover how to test each family, and the point of testing is to turn a vague pile into things you trust.

Some boards are worth more as lessons than as parts. A well-laid-out supply or a clever mechanism rewards study even when there's nothing on it to pull. Let those teach you, then move the board on.

## How to use this guide

The guide has four parts, and you don't need to read them in order. Foundations, the section you're in now, covers what applies everywhere: staying safe, setting up a bench, and the core techniques for getting parts off a board cleanly. Read the safety page before you open anything that plugs into the wall.

The Donor Guides go device by device. Each one tells you what's inside a given kind of junk, a printer, an ATX supply, a microwave, what's worth pulling, how to get it out, and what can hurt you on the way. Start here when you've got a specific dead thing in front of you and want to know whether it's worth opening.

The Components section goes part by part. When you've pulled something and want to identify it, test it, and know whether it's any good, this is where to look. Each page covers one family, resistors, capacitors, MOSFETs, relays, and so on, with the markings to read and the meter checks to run.

The Projects section puts salvaged parts to work in small builds: a continuity tester, a bench supply from a dead PC, a discharge tool. Use them as targets that tell you what to keep an eye out for while you're tearing down.

In practice you'll bounce between all four. A dead microwave sends you to its donor guide, which sends you to the safety page before you touch the HV cap, then to the components pages to test what you pulled, and maybe to a project once you've got the parts for it. The Theory links scattered through, pointing into Open Circuits, are there for when you want the underlying electronics rather than the salvage procedure.
